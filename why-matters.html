Why Our Work Matters

Closing the Gap

Millions of children in India are silently struggling with learning differences. We are working to change that.

The Challenge

A Silent Crisis in Education

India has over 350 million students in its education system. Of these, at least 35 million children are likely to be silently struggling with learning challenges—not because they lack intellectual ability, but because they learn differently.

At the same time, classrooms are often under-resourced, with teachers supporting at least 50 students per class. Without awareness or training, many of these children are overlooked or misunderstood—often labelled as ‘slow’ or ‘careless.’

Over time, the gap widens. Children fall behind. Confidence erodes. Potential is lost.

The Reality

What Children Face

Most schools in India do not yet have access to early screening, trained specialists, or inclusive teaching practices.

Children with dyslexia may struggle to read fluently. Children with dyscalculia may find numbers overwhelming. Children with ADHD may find it impossible to sit still in a traditional classroom.

Without identification and support, these children are left without the tools they need to thrive—despite having the intelligence and potential to succeed.

Our Response

How We Are Making a Difference

Neurowise Academy Foundation works to close this gap through awareness, capacity building, and direct student support.

Building Awareness

Helping schools, families, and communities understand that learning differences are real, common, and treatable—not a reflection of a child's intelligence.

Equipping Educators

Training teachers in inclusive strategies so they can recognise and support neurodivergent learners within their everyday classroom practice.

Supporting Children

Working directly with students through screening, assessment, and tailored interventions—ensuring every child gets the support they need to thrive.

“Every child deserves to be understood. Learning differently is not a deficit—it is a difference that, when recognised and supported, can become a strength.”
